Bai Xiao Chun is a rather inconsistent character who cowardly tries to avoid conflict above anything else. Throughout the show, he prefers to beg and flatter his way out of problems, and if that fails, he surrenders or runs. In fact, his whole motivation is to live as long as possible, and that's why he trains to be a sage-level cultivator in the first place, on the promises of immortality. This is all due to a traumatic event in his childhood, when his parents tell him that he must live on. While the show never fails to capitalize on the comedic factor of these moments, sometimes it could get frustrating, as if the narrative never progresses, because the protagonist is just trying as hard as he could to dodge all advancement.

However, there are a few times throughout the 52 episodes when Bai Xiao Chun is pushed into a corner and forced to fight. And here, he reveals that he's actually a genius martial artist that dominates enemies two levels higher than he is, despite never really having trained to fight in stress tested situations. He switches from silly to killer in a moment's trigger. Don't get me wrong. He trains hard in various combat skills. But it's hard to imagine him being good at fighting when he spends his life avoiding it. When he does fight, Yi Nian Yong Heng (A Will Eternal) shows off a brilliant high budget animation, one of the few 2D Chinese animations, and with very well directed action sequences that had me literally on the edge of my seat. As soon as I tell myself it's about to get good, our Student Bai goes back to his silly self.

Really, if there's any flaw with this show, it's the pacing. Despite being a cultivation show, it's really unlike any other. Instead of constant bullying and being forced to fend for one's life, the focus is, for the most part, more on training. Bai Xiao Chun spends the vast majority of the show either practicing his pill alchemy, making high level medicine for people, or practicing various cultivation skills and discerning their true meaning. Unlike in other cultivation shows, where the leveling are just vessels to push conflict, the leveling seems to be the whole point in Yi Nian Yong Heng. Therefore, to my dismay, the genre seems to be more of comedy and world building, with a little bit of adventure and slice of life, than an all out action-adventure archplot, which is what I wanted it to be. Despite so, I was able to appreciate its strengths for what they are. And while the moments between the action drag a bit -- let's be honest, they never, ever get as bad as standard bearing Japanese shounen anime like One Piece.

I will say that the comedy is rather funny, but it also happens that I sense it is stalling the plot, and I end up not enjoying it as much. And the training, despite it being drawn out, is actually one of the strengths of Chinese donghua, since unlike most anime, it actually shows serious attempts to train. In his spare time, Bai Xiao Chun is found reading books, memorizing herbs and their uses, practicing alchemy (which is a bit like blacksmithing, except with magical fire and herbal ingredients instead of weapons), and doing physical training to increase his level. In other words, he never skips out on training to sneak in to other people's summer training camps.

Anyways, take it for what you would, but while I would prefer that Bai Xiao Chun steps up to the plate more often so that the main plot points could move forward, I could also appreciate how the protagonist is spending his time to increase his level to a degree.

The other reviews mention a harem, but there really isn't one (although he ends up with one in the novel). The protagonist meets a couple girls that are potential romantic interests, but there is always one obviously primary interest, and never more than one girl per arc. The girls in prior arcs have a place in the story, but don't interact with Bai Xiao Chun much. If you're looking for it, you could convince yourself that the protagonist has it in the bag, but that is hardly the focus of the show. Except for main girl, none of the girls respect Bai Xiao Chun much for the majority of the arcs.

If you're looking for a good all-rounder with a more relaxed pacing, perhaps a bit of slice of life before the main plot picks up, then I'd easily recommend Yi Nian Yong Heng. But if you're looking for a power fantasy, you'd be sorely disappointed. While there are fight scenes to show Bai Xiao Chun's progress, most of the time he's coming up with petty little schemes to get out of jail free.

My current review is based of the first 4 episodes that I've watched.

I have to say the anime really blew my mind. It was much better than what I initially expected.

Story: 7/10

Having read the novel, I would say this anime is quite faithful following the original plot of the novel.

A Will Eternal tells the story of a simple village boy, through a fateful encounter, gained the opportunity to step onto the path to become an Immortal. The first season depicts the life of the boy in an Immortal Sect, it shows the various workings of the Sect, the people and the locations within. It is extremely interesting to watch.

One particular thing which could be done better would be the pacing of the story, which I feel is a bit too quick. It does seem like the director really skimmed through a lot of things.

Art: 9/10

The artworks and animation of this series are especially pleasing to the eye. The background arts are truly amazing, and I can't get enough of them.

The important characters are all well designed, and the action scenes are all fluid and well animated. The reason why I didn't rate it a 10 would be I think a lot of the background/side characters could have been more varied and distinct.

Sound: 8/10

The background music, voice acting, general sound effects are all top notch. There's not much I can comment on, but I do think there can still be room for improvements.

Character: 7/10

I think this anime depicted the majority of the characters, their personalities and interactions very much on point. The characters have all been faithful to the novel.

I do think some of the characters are a bit bland and seem very 1-dimensional, but this can only be attributed to the novel itself. Though it did made me lower my ratings a bit for this section.

Enjoyment: 9/10

So far, I very much enjoyed watching this anime. It is really humorous, and there are enough actions to keep me interested.

Overall: 8/10

Overall, I would definitely recommend for those who haven't yet watched. It would definitely be a surprise for many, I would think.

It is harem. It is romance. Once again, MAL, you've done me in with your wrong tagging. I hope someone will have corrected the tags by the time this review is up.

There are a handful of things I've logged in to say here:

Just to state the obvious, this anime is targeted at Chinese audiences. It is based off a novel by the same name (of which you may find more detailed information on Novelupdates). The directors obviously didn't intend to include the English audiences as part of their target audience, so to watch it, you have to have a certain degree of knowledge on the Xianxia troupe -- which is basically people cultivating their Qi to various levels so as to achieve immortality.

Do not expect to go in without that background knowledge and come away with any new knowledge. You'll just be miserably lost.

(And as usual, if you have to read an entire novel just to understand the plot of an anime, is that even worth watching? I mean, the whole goal of an anime is to draw new audiences in. I can't even--)

Even if you have knowledge of the xianxia troupe, there is still a chance you might strike this anime off your list, and for good reasons at that.

Just for background knowledge, the source material had 1250+ chapters and got axed in the end. The beginning is naturally slow-paced, as the author spent much time developing the world around the MC's sect, comrades etcetera. I myself had watched this anime at 2x speed, and even then, it is only because of the rather dry season this time that I even stayed throughout the mundane opening. The middle section, however, was what kept me going.

I didn't expect much from this show. You see, the Chinese anime industry has had a record of lazy directing for a long time now. For all of bilibili's bold proclamations at the start of the year that the Chinese anime industry would overtake Japan's, their shows clearly displayed a fundamentally lacking concept - that just taking popular novels with existing fanbases and adapting them would be enough. None of them have even tried to make an appeal to attract new fanbases. Tbh, even MDZS suffered tremendously from that problem in its S2.

But surprisingly, where the first half of the this anime lured me in into this false sense of security that everything is just another 'slice of life' troupe, it sure grabbed me by my non-existent balls and kept me at the edge of my seat near the beginning of the second half. All of a sudden, we were just dropped into this dangerous situation where the protagonist's OP halo may not work for once, and episode after episode, grueling minute after minute, we were just forced to bear witness to the suffocating struggle for survival by the protagonist and his seniors in a hopeless situation. It was long and tiresome, but really, it was because my heart kept racing throughout, feeling the exhaustion, desperation, and hopelessness my guys were feeling.

I even shed a few tears here and there.

Then. Well. After the memorial episode where the MC was honoured for his sacrifices, we're just dumped a scene of the MC spending time at a pavilion with his senior, flirting, KISSING. I just- I zoned out there. MAL, you dropped the ball in tagging. Honestly, how could you even, with the tags already on NU all spelled out for you? But what disappointed me most was the show. It completely dropped the ball with that sudden romance scene that came out of nowhere. Not only did it trample upon all the respect we as readers had developed for one of the characters, it completely trashed both the MC and love interest's characterizations, making them seem no more than flimsy, typical anime troupes who are even somewhat scrummy.

I thought that was the worst until I carried on to the next episode. Lo and behold, a budding romance with the junior disciple sister (not by blood, but having knelt before the same Master) who goes around call the MC 'Big Brother' and the MC who started flirting with her despite his lingering interest for the first female lead.

Yeah. I'm done.

I'm done with this show, and I'll drop it here, because I fear watching anymore would simply ruin what good memories I had of those precious few episodes in the middle.

If I had known it was harem, I wouldn't even have given it a chance to begin with, so good job, MAL???

For what it's worth, I'd have given it an 9 just to give this show the attention it deserved, until the whole harem thing happened. The characters were cute, even if the thievery and flagrant manipulation by the MC were mildly annoying, and that middle arc was simply phenomenal in its execution.

Harem can be good when done well, but with that preview of how badly harem is going to sabotage this entire anime, all I can say about this show is really... 'What a pity.'

*Note: As of 8/20/2020, there are no subs for this out yet. This is based off of my rudimentary Mandarin skills and my having read the original novel (in its English TL).

I've been excited for Yi Nian Yong Heng (YNYH) since its announcement for two major reasons: A), the the studio creating this did Mo Dao Zu Shi, and B), I enjoyed the original novel that this is based on. Unfortunately, I can say without any doubt that this adaptation has failed to fulfill the precedents set by both its original novel as well as the studio's previous masterworks.

Story:

5/10

The story of YNYH is rather simple at its premise, unlike what the vague description on MAL would tell you. The setting is that of archetypal Chinese immortal-myth, filled with sects of cultivators, flying swords, and heart demons: The classic and quintessential Xianxia worldbuilding. Our protagonist is Bai Xiaochun, a boy from a minor village with a cowardly personality who fears death immensely. His aim is to become an immortal, and to this end, he joins the Spirit Stream Sect, becoming and training as a cultivator. The earlier parts of YNYH depict him interacting and experiencing the world of immortals, specifically his struggles with a particular pompous elder disciple.

I have no complaints for the initial story of YNYH, except for one thing: The pacing.

It's not great.

Everything that happens does so in a blur. It's not so much that problem of the speed of the story so much as it actually is the pacing of the scenes. Events happen in flashes and cuts are absolutely mindbogglingly bad and seemingly random at times. There is a great overuse of anime speed lines in the corners and edges of the screen, and composition as a whole is pretty bad. All this is only made worse with the duration of each episode; none of the episodes last longer than twenty-or-so minutes, including the openings and endings, which even by themselves are much longer than average. As a whole, this makes the sequences feel fragmented and incomplete many times.

Art:

6/10

Art in this show ranges from great to bad. Starting off from character designs, it's clear that this was done by the same studio as Mo Dao Zu Shi. Characters have an excessively anime look that would normally conflict with the often realistic-looking backgrounds, but somehow don't. You could insert any of these characters into MDZS and vice-versa and they would look interchangeable.

However, the CG cannot be said to be the same.

Whereas in MDZS CG is masterfully placed and edited to the point of being near-indistinguishable from the anime parts (or just really pretty to the point of being *better* than most 2d-art in anime), in YNYH, CG is glaringly obvious and ugly at many times. Characters look awkward in many shots when surrounded by CG environments, and special effects range from decent to extremely ugly. One example, near 10:16 of episode 1, has dust that I'm pretty sure is just a looped transparent gif taken from real-life and completely unedited. With this being said though, the energy effects in the fights and use of magic generally look pretty good throughout.

Sound:

7/10

No complaints here. Sound design is actually quite a bit above average, and I love the MC's voice acting - it's clear that they share the same voice actor as Wei Wuxian from MDZS, which I know I've used as a comparison many times already, but I can't stop from making parallels between them. Very nice overall.

Character:

7/10

Bai Xiaochun = Wei Wuxian but younger. I love his scared and cowardly disposition and his humorous antics. In my opinion, this is enough to elevate the characters up to a seven-or-eight out-of-ten (where my average is five) simply because he's so great, even if all the characters are just archetypes so far.

Enjoyment/Overall:

Enjoyment - 7/10

Overall - 6/10

Despite my rambling, I consider YNYH to be pretty enjoyable - a seven-out-of-ten for me. I like the designs, the MC, and the special effects for the energy and magic and fighting enough to accept the glaring issues with art, especially when I look at it by itself rather in comparison to MDZS. I'd recommend it if you know Chinese (at least as of I created this review, when there are no subs out) and have been wanting for more cultivation stuff. If you don't know anything about cultivation, however, I would recommend either watching Mo Dao Zu Shi to get a primer on the topic (since that show isn't paced as quickly as this one) or searching up a guide.
