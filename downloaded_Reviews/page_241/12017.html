"What is anime?" A question many have asked and answered, but what is it really? Today I will answer that question, as well as give some insight as to why you should (not) watch Nami, which ironically enough can't be considered anime. Without further ado, let's begin!

Anime as we know it today is a visual storytelling medium. Consisting of multiple key factors like story, art, animation, characters and sound, the sky's the limit for what can be produced. That does not come without its shortcomings though, whether it's bad directing, faults in the story, jarring art/animation or poor sound, there's always something you can detract from the overall quality of an anime. Even some of the most renowned shows like Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood have flaws that can pull them down. However the topic of this review is not the most renowned anime ever, but quite the opposite, one of the worst "anime" (can I really call it an anime?) ever created. Today I will take a look at the not-so critically acclaimed "Nami".

Raise your hand if you've ever heard this one: A woman is pleasuring herself in her room, when all of a sudden a fish appears out of nowhere and slides inside of her. Raise your hand again if you expected hundreds, if not thousands of other fish to appear and slide inside the woman aswell. If you raised your hand to either of these situations, then congratulations, you've probably watched Nami. The fish appear from everywhere, from inside the TV, out of walls, and even places you didn't know fish could be (not like any of the places I mentioned are locations where fish are usually at anyway.)

With the head-scratching synopsis for Nami out of the way, let's move on to something less...strange, if you will. Don't get me wrong, I don't mean less strange in the form of it not being weird, but less strange as in you will at least understand what's going on, as opposed to the wild synopsis. This less strange thing is in fact animation, the very backbone to an anime in many people's opinion. While good animation isn't necessary for an anime to be successful, it can certainly help build up tension or ambiance, depending on how it's done. In Nami however, what you see is what you get, and it is piss poor at that. From censorship (REALLY? Not this again) to awful movements from both the woman and the fish, this is definitely some of the worst "animation" I've ever seen.

If you thought the animation was bad, just wait until you hear the sound, it's beyond horrible. The sounds the fish make are definitely some of the weirdest sounds I've ever heard. It's like a group of people slurping up something liquidy, except it's much worse and much louder. I had to turn my sound down while watching due to it being painful to listen to.

I didn't think it was possible to find something worse than Pupa or Utsu Musume Sayuri, but Nami blew me out of the water with how unfathomable and awful it is. I actually watched Nami twice, because I didn't understand it at first. While I did end up gaining some very basic understanding for it, having to experience it not only once, but twice was an incredibly straining task that I wish I hadn't done, even if it "only" took 6 minutes in total. Nami actually has ZERO value, something I never thought I would say ever. Don't watch this, you're better off doing absolutely nothing.

Nami tells the daring story about a young woman who works as a hooker. When we are first introduced to the main character, she is sitting down on a mattress; seemingly performing some sort of sex act. By this point it is unclear whether she is doing it for her own personal enjoyment; or if she's aware about the fact that she is a fictional character (much like Deadpool or the Muppets), by which case it is possible that she is performing for the audience.

We are then introduced to the first of many side characters; a fish. The creature doesn't seem to be affected by the fact that it is crawling on dry land, even though that usually isn't the natural habitat for a water based animal. This leads me to believe that the fish is magical; even though the anime has neither the "magic" or "fantasy" tag attached to it.

The fish then proceeds to enter the main character's womanhood; though his motivation for doing so are very unclear thanks to poor writing. Because of this we don't really care about whatever fate awaits him within the main character's body. This could've been easily fixed by fleshing out his backstory a bit further; instead his character arc just comes across as rushed and confused.

Next a lot more fish start to appear. They all follow the first fish, which means that he was probably the leader (this furthers my last point). I also believe that the fish are a metaphore, a clever play on the common saying "there are more fish in the sea" in reference to potential mating partners. Since the woman let's every fish in the room enter her, I assume she's some sort of prostitute.

The art is extremly realistic, and really the strong point of the anime. The sound design also gets a pass. But where this show's flaws start to appear is with the characters. We never learn anything concrete about any of them, which in turn made me care about them almost as little as the cast of Madoka Magica. The story is also very vague, and it's short runtime doesn't exactly help either.

In the end, "Nami" is a show that has a lot of potential, but never succeeds in becoming anything more than mediocre. I've heard that the character motivations were a lot more clear in the original manga, something that's been lost in the adaptation. But if you're a fan of indie projects, or that SAO episode in which Asuna and Kirito goes fishing, you'll probably enjoy it.

I have been on a "quest" to find very bizarre and surreal anime lately. My first encounter with that sort of stuff was Utsu Musume Sayuri, and it was hands down one of the worst things I had ever watched. It was plain repulsive and made me indescribably disturbed as I was watching it. In a sense, Nami isn't much different. It's almost as weird as Sayuri, and in some cases, even weirder (though definitely not as disturbing).

Nami starts off pretty "normally". For the first few seconds, we see a woman pleasing herself with her fingers in bed. Well, it's still quite weird, but it's not something to write home about. I've seen plenty of way weirder shit.

Don't worry, though, as it only goes downhill from here.

The rest of Nami is what I would like to call "porn made for people who are obsessed with fish". And I think that title is very befitting considering what happens next. After a while, a fish appears out of nowhere. Then, while making disturbing slurping noises, guess what it does? It literally slides inside her genitalia at a rapid pace. Yes, literally.

This doesn't stop here, though. After a while, hundreds of fish, or even thousands, start appearing out of nowhere and enter the woman's genitalia as well, while she's lying there in an unconscious state. And let me tell you, these fish are fucking fast. So fast that they could probably beat Usain Bolt in a race. We also get close-ups of the fish entering the woman's genitalia. And surprise, surprise! These close-ups are extremely disturbing.

Nami concludes leaving everything completely ambiguous (not like it wasn't ambiguous enough already). As we are about to hit the three-minute mark, it suddenly cuts to a black screen and the closing credits start rolling. And thank God, this abomination then comes to an end after what it felt like one of the longest three minutes of my life.

By looking at Nami through rose-tinted glasses, few might find deeper meaning and substance behind it, and say it's a metaphor for life and how it knocks you down when you are simply enjoying it, or all that crap. Though, as it stands to me, and I believe to most others, Nami is nothing but an atrocity that should never have been created. But in the end, it's up to you to decide whether it's meaningful or not.
