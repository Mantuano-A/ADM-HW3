Honestly this is review is not free of bias, I watched this show when I was five and I absolutely loved it as a kid. I will do my best effort to be not biased, but do take this review with a grain of salt.

Story:
The story is pretty simple. Our hero gets possessed by an alien who calls himself Ultraman and he goes around and beats up Kaiju that threaten Earth. The story does have progression despite how it might seem at first. I cannot say much because spoilers, but the ending is pretty spectacular. However, getting there can take lots of patience. You won't miss much if you skip most of the ordinary kaiju fights.

Art:
This series was made in 1980 so don't expect the clean artwork you see today. Perspective can become pretty jarring and some things aren't particularly well fleshed out. Fight scenes are lacking compared to today's standards.

Sound:
The opening and ending is amazing (bias). But they are without a doubt, pretty catchy. The sounds effects are pretty crude and old, but its pretty good if you can get past that.

Character:
Honestly, characters are pretty two dimensional. There isn't much character development, but the show really likes to bait people into the typical "He's gonna finally reveal his identity!" card. Pig the robot is pretty charming despite looking nothing like a pig nor is he visually appealing. Marume becomes less of a jerk. Other than that none of the humans particularly grow.

Final Thoughts:
I wouldn't recommend this to anyone who is fed up with Power Rangers or anyone who don't like Godzilla like stuff.

The Ultraman is your generic classic "monster of the week" show, so be prepared for the repetitiveness.

Story: Mysterious creature monsters appearing from nowhere, Ultraman appears from space and warns human, he "attach" himself to the protagonist and tells him not reveal identity because "he will know the truth later" .
It stops there, no progression, the whole story itself is an excuse for more and more battles.

Characters: They are all adults but also act maturely, which is rare in anime nowadays, the problem with this anime is how it handles its characters, the writer knows how create appealing characters in terms of personality, but fails at character development within the story, I have four examples,

Episode 2: Even though the captain appears cold and stern, he genuinely cares for his crew, he did not arrive at the party either because he is an unsocial introvert or because he is a very busy man. Still, he sends a gift to Mutsumi.
Episode 3: Marume was depicted as a stupid repulsive comic relief fool, but i turns out he has a soft side, and actually is brave and witty.
Episode 6: Although typically laid back, Tobe is an avid engineer, he loves the maddock and is very proud and too passionate of his creation.
Episode 7: Pig seemed like a one-dimensional supporting robot character, actually he is an old robot who is aware of his existence and questions his current value in the modern era.

Notice the common denominator in these examples? none of them is related to the plot or exploring themes.

I don't care about the Maddock! and I sure don't care about how Tobe care about the Maddock, this has little to no value about the story and story themes.
This is NOT "character development", it is considered "characterisation", unless this was a storyless slice of life drama anime, which is not.
They feel they are in the wrong show, and should have been in a slice of life special military force anime.

Sure, this helps in characterisation (building a personality), but that's not a good writing if a whole episode lacks providing information about characters in context with the story or its themes.

The story is not about the fighter jet, nor about social relation between military squads, the story is about Ultraman, and the mysterious creature appearing from nowhere. Ultraman himself is silent for no reason other than "you will know the truth later" and doesn't reveal anything about his origins or the monsters.

They could at least develop those characters by telling us the reason they joined this special squad, so far only Mutsumi said she's interested in this mystery, but she doesn't progress further than that, and make further research, such as trying to study Ultraman. Do these monsters mainly attack Japan? There is an upnormal lack of international voice, intervention, and politics. The writer could've introduced those themes and elements to use them for character development.

Only episode 8 was decent because picks up the story progression slightly, as we learn more about the how much the enemy knows about Hikari and Ultraman. The media shows concerns about Ultraman's origins, and an introduction of intellegent talking scheming monsters now which is more interesting.

But then, unfortunately the show goes again to its usual self. I think if it had been shorter, it will be more entertaining to watch.
