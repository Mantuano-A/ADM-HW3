While it was airing, Shiguang Dailiren was considered the hidden gem of Spring 2021 by many fans. Now, with its strong finale and its pass to a second season, its insane rise in score and its entry to MAL's top 25 anime will make more people watch it and discuss it. But is it really worth the hype?

First of all, let's state the obvious and answer this question to a certain audience. People with an interest in non sci-fi time traveling shows could easily enjoy this. Flashy and with style, the protagonists use photographs to enter a past moment of the same world's timeline, and control others' bodies and minds there for a few hours with their main objective being the solution of criminal cases. Using that power, they must be careful not to change the flow of events because the butterfly effect may lead the world to a completely different future. How do they handle situations like these?

Well, Cheng Xiaoshi's character is heavily inspired by the mad scientist from Steins;Gate, Okabe Rintarou. In his "diving operations", he gets emotional when he faces hardships and he feels the urge to help other people. He never puts the blame in others and tries to take full responsibility, and that's what could make him a tragic character or... a hero. His partner, Lu Guang is more grounded and that makes sense because he is an observer when they use their special power. Sadly, in these 11 episodes all the other characters are shallow and forgettable, so in the end, only Cheng matters.
The dialogue and character interactions are a big minus in this show. There's sometimes fan service, while at times there are just tragic events happening and people acting immaturely. Of course, not everyone views this as a flaw.

The animation and art show almost no similarity to those of common anime, and that's because Shiguang Dailiren is a donghua. Their style is closer to a live webtoon than a japanese anime. As for the soundtrack, it's a good reason to watch the show; the beautiful and romantic opening song, the dynamic and mysterious ending song that makes you want to watch more, but most of the insert songs too; all of them are praiseworthy.

In my point of view, to really answer the question I set in my prologue, Link Click is neither innovative nor brilliant. Its selling points are either easy to use or already popular in fiction: simple time travel with no sci-fi approach, a compassionate main character, simple life lessons and morals that say no to greed, attractive looking characters and shallow drama that stems from bad life choices. It's like a failed attempt to make a crossover of Sherlock Holmes and Steins;Gate.

Thank you for reading.

Hopefully this review will not be overly confusing. I burned a lot of brain cells (as if I even had any) as I wrote this so there will be errors, and there will be times when I contradict myself. Please feel free to send me any suggestions and critiques.

TL;DR: this show has some good and bad aspects, but I believe the good outweigh the bad and hence my score. As for whether you should invest your time on this, if you are looking for something new, a fresh perspective maybe, and if you like an episodic story with emotional ups and downs - this is the show for you. Okay.

I think I’m going to start this review with a generic statement on life that will make me seem smart. Here goes.

Photographs are the true windows into lives - the continuous string of moments and experiences that make them up, and just as diverse as each person. Ultimately, it is the photograph which showcases what truly makes us human. Among its other ideas, this show reminds us of the importance of these momentary captures to every life.

(Whew, that was out of the way. Alright.)

Shiguang Dailiren, or Link Click, is an anthology of short stories centered around a main mystery plot. The main plot deals with how Cheng Xiaoshi and Lu Guang, the main duo, attempt to resolve the many challenges that come with ‘diving’ into photos to time travel to their clients’ past. By contrast, each short story or arc shows us immersive, somewhat slice-of-life-like, glimpses into these clients’ many different lives. These arcs are emotional - some makes you cry, some warms and melts your heart. Seems like a pretty nice story so far.

I will address several aspects of the storyline: cohesiveness of logic, clarity, and general direction. I will then address issues with the show’s style of storytelling, such as its inconsistent sense of agency, reliance on the shock factor, and something else.

For the first one, I have to first mention the basis for the story’s premise, and that is time travel. Time travel is a pretty difficult trope to work with in general, since the genre itself tends to ruin many stories with the amount of plot holes it can give rise to. Link Click’s story specifically deals with the non-loop type of time travel, the one in which you change the past yourself and not because your actions in the past cause you to change the past in the first place. (Get a load of that.) This kind of time travel can be especially problematic as it means that instances of time travel are random and spontaneous and that the narrative has to account for their myriad butterfly effects. With that stated, it becomes clear that Link Click is already treading into dangerous territory with its basic premise. Regarding its execution of the premise, the show fortunately tries its best to cover up any plot holes and make its plot as self-contained as it can be, whether or not those holes are caused by the concept of time travel or not. Several of my concerns about inconsistencies along the course of the show managed to be resolved in later episodes. However, there will always be some kind of glitch you will find, and there isn’t always a satisfying but complex explanation that can deal with it. Nevertheless, I consider this deficiency of the show to be somehow inherent to its genre, and I take the stance that penalizing Link Click further for this is ultimately meaningless.

Clarity ties into the cohesion of logic, but it’s separate because it also addresses the background of the plot. Questions remain about the origin and mechanics of the storyline, such as how did the photo-diving venture get started and how the superpower really works. But for fiction in general, not everything has to be explained. All that stuff forming the ‘peripheral structure’ of the central story, such as world mechanics, kickstarts the general sequence of events to be presented to our imagination, and since it is the latter which a narrative focuses on, the 'structure' need not be expounded on in great detail. In fact, there’s the risk that over-explanation and magnification of these behind-the-scenes specifics can result in an incoherent narrative. As for Link Click, originally this ambiguity about its peripheral structure proves to be beneficial to its plot, but as season two has been announced I can’t help but be irritated that the producers decided to elongate the story to explain some background and thus create the impression that the original story in this first season is riddled with holes. In addition, the fact that new rules to the superpower of photo-diving are introduced as the story goes on makes it seem like they are devised on a whim to advance the plot. On the other hand, an exposition dump at the very beginning of the show fully explaining the different rules doesn’t sound quite good either, but it could have worked with creative adjustments. Overall, clarity is something Link Click might have to work on a little bit, but, granted, it’s already treading on a very fine line.

For me, a story’s overall sense of direction is highly important when I try to evaluate it. What is a story moving towards? A resolution of a predicament? A revelation of truth? A story should make this direction clear throughout so that it can arrive at a solid endpoint. Only with this will a story have a good sense of purpose and meaning, even if it is just about fighting bad guys or hunting treasure. For Link Click, its episodic nature with semi-standalone episodes make this overall sense of direction somewhat muddled. The repeated back-and-forth transitions into the short stories diminished the clear focus on the narrative’s sense of direction to the point where I nearly forgot what the entire show is about during one arc. One big counterargument regarding my point is that not all stories have to have a sense of direction. Slice-of-life and comedy shows may not rely so much on the big picture as on momentary details. I do think this view is valid. Perhaps it is the story’s intent to present us with smaller pictures of the story along with a more expansive one. However, it’s still true that the transition between the overall and smaller arcs could have been better, at least to the point where the viewer’s mind does not get muddled.

Now, finally onto Link Click’s style of storytelling: how its events move along in a sequential order. This ties into the story’s pacing, tone, and atmosphere, and it is essential to how immersive the story is. Broadly speaking, I’d say that the pacing of the narrative as a whole is inconsistent and maybe irritating, while the pacing inside most of the short stories is good. Because the individual arcs can get quite slow at times, you can feel that they ‘clash’ with the main mystery arc, mainly because the shifts back and forth feel disjointed and can take you by surprise. If you just look at some of the small arcs themselves, however, you will find that their pacing is comparatively more immersive, so much that it wouldn’t hurt for them to be standalone stories of their own. But back to the drawback of the overall pacing: the show not only took the risk of presenting this large-story-filled-with-smaller-ones narrative model but also failed to properly account for its deficiencies. The show does try to account for the model’s deficiencies, though, which leads us into the next section.

The show relies too much on cheap shock value, primarily with its use of cliffhangers. I just mentioned that pacing problem, and it turns out that, on multiple occasions (almost every episode), Link Click, after a pretty slow and heartwarming mini-story, would choose to speed up the pacing by showing us a BANG! cliffhanger at the very end of the episode. This technique of Link Click is so overused that I had to talk about this problem in this wholly separate section. The story tries to fool viewers with heart-stopping scenes (granted, they are pretty good at stopping your heart) just before an absolute cliff of an ending into believing that the plot is good, rather than doing a gradual build up of tension that would have benefited the mystery part of the show. If you really think about this cliffhanger thingy in Link Click, it should seem very suspicious to you that it’s relied upon literally all the time to advance the main plot instead of maybe some variety. And it’s no wonder the cliffhangers might be one of the more problematic aspects of the show.

A separate characteristic of the show that bothers me is how it evidently tries to resemble an anime. For instance, when characters argue or are exasperated, floating bubbles appear above their head or lines emanate from a building. Also, those distinctive anime facial expressions, like > _ < or • _ • (where the eyes are noticeably small circles instead of normal size), are rare in the show, but I think I saw one or two instances of them. My point is that the show’s decision to include these elements in order to convey stuff like character interactions is a bad choice. Obviously it decides to show these to make something seem very funny, but to me this backfires immensely because the show now has moods that vary too much: you have this very serious portrayal of psychological issues and you have these kawaii-like moments. These moods aren’t often shown back to back, but the fact that they exist in the same story makes your head spin around a little. Not only this, but the story also has to rely on prior and outside knowledge now, since non-anime fans usually don’t associate different types of anime faces with specific emotions. Not that non-anime fans might watch this but still. You might object that this characteristic of inconsistent moods exists in many other franchises and that I shouldn’t criticize Link Click for this, but the fact that the show had potential to construct a well-planned mood around its plot and then suddenly present us with the aforementioned distinctively anime scenes leads me with no choice but to ultimately penalize it.

At last (sigh that was long), onto the characters. There’s really only one main character in the whole show, and that’s your black-haired hot boy Cheng Xiaoshi. The other two individuals in the photo studio are flat personality-wise (in this season, at least) and pretty much count as stock characters. Cheng Xiaoshi is the only person that gets relatively extensive coverage about his background, and throughout the course of the story he changes from this annoying dude that everything hates to being much more self-aware about his flaws. With only eleven episodes in this show, perhaps it is only natural that meaningful character development is limited to this black-haired boy. I must add that the specific scene when Cheng Xiaoshi develops is quite well presented and unique, and it definitely speaks volumes about the hidden talent that the producers have but unfortunately fail to utilize fully. I didn’t actually talk that much about characters, probably because this aspect is more straightforward than the plot.

Besides all that about the plot and characters, I now detail my thoughts on the story’s message and overall purpose. It is clear that the story is mainly of the entertainment type, one that grabs you by the neck and forces you to watch several episodes at a time. It’s about the good guys fighting the bad guy(s). But there’s two good underlying meanings behind Link Click’s facade which I appreciate. Normally, time travel stories demonstrate that time travel is necessary to solve all your problems. You have this in Steins;Gate and ERASED. But Link Click introduces something new by also saying that time travel is not as useful in certain contexts. Cheng Xiaoshi famously (? lmao) states that sometimes, even stepping into people’s shoes back in time won’t solve anything. This message, even though obvious, is truly something fresh that Link Click passes on to us. Because of this ineffectiveness, sometimes it is best to leave the power of time travel untouched, and now we stumble upon a more familiar theme. The fact that Link Click bothers to show its reasoning leading up to this more iconic don’t-touch-anything-too-powerful message deserves praise. Onto the other message. This one is kind of meta since it directly connects to us. Remember what I’ve said at the beginning of this review about how photos are the gateway to what makes us human? Link Click, through the concept of photo-diving, shows that photos are quite literally gateways into what makes someone, someone. It repeatedly shows how photos are reminders of important moments and experiences in one’s life, and that we should look at photos to gain a better understanding of ourselves and to move forward in life. At the very end of the extra episode 5.5, there is a sequence of montages in which real-life photos showing key memories of Internet users are depicted one by one. This sequence ends with the statement that when daily moments, be it happy or sad or of any other emotion, enter a camera, the resulting photograph shows how we are remarkable in each of our ways and that we should not be afraid to show photographs, to show the true side of ourselves to others. Now, that’s a great message. It really striked home for me and made a lasting impression. They say that we as humans know a lot of things but we forget some of them from time to time. This message is one I’ve forgotten, and I thank Link Click for reminding me about the importance of photographs to one’s memories and, quite frankly, one’s being.

About the art, I have nothing but general praise. Lighting and color were great, and backgrounds were consistent in their level of detail. CGI is really sparse and not distracting. Quite a few of the shots have a reflective quality to them, especially ones with flat surfaces like floors and tabletops. The basketball game particularly stood out, and so much that I recommend watching that game just by itself even if you are not planning to finish the entire show. A minor nitpick would be that characters’ faces look slightly different in their shape and layout between far-away and close-up shots, resulting in a hint of inconsistent art that some might call ugly.

I would have not thought to comment on the voice acting for any show, but since Link Click only has its original dub I must say that the voice acting is noticeably average and probably even bad. The voice actors attempt to include a fair bit of emotion into their lines, but any chance it might move our soul is consistently nullified by the fact that you can TELL the actors are in a recording studio. It’s not so much the echos and any outside noise but more so the shallow depth of the sound waves that make the dialogue sound somewhat distant and electronic, if you get what I mean. During several moments I just had to roll my eyes because of how cheesy some lines sounded due to this sound quality.

Lastly, I cannot help but gush about the opening and ending songs and sequences. All of them are pretty cool, not least the animated finger-tutting in the opening. They have really been a joy to watch and hear.

This review was truly difficult to write, and this show difficult to rate as well. In the end, I settled for 6 / 10, which is definitely above average for me but maybe low for you. I must stress and re-stress the fact that a rating is nothing much more than a mere number, and what matters is that YOU watch it and YOU experience all that this show makes you go through. For me, that 6 was what my deeper innate instinct was (wow so romantic) - you could definitely say that it’s arbitrary. And that’s what individual scores really are. Different people feel different ways about a show and then proceed to hammer a number that they felt was right onto their thoughts.

[spoiler-free preliminary review]

Have you ever wondered what it would be like to go into someone else's past? Feeling their emotions and reliving their experiences, could you really stop yourself from doing things differently and fixing their regrets?

Story: 9
The director of this donghua(chinese anime) is Li Haoling, who also did the story and direction for Flavors of Youth/Shikioriori (and also Heaven Official's Blessing). There are similar sentimental themes in Link Click, and the episodes do a great job of showing us the deep emotion rooted in things that are seemingly mundane. It even draws inspiration from real events. It pulls at viewer heartstrings and features unexpected twists and turns. There is still a lot of mystery to be uncovered so far, and I'm super hyped to see it unfold. The story also explores the consequences of traveling back in time(butterfly effect), as well as the morality of changing the past.

Art: 9
The character designs are very stylish (as you can see from the cover), and the clothing styles are quite realistic, representative of what an average person would wear. The backgrounds are very well-drawn, and the watercolor backgrounds are simply beautiful.
However, I can't say it's a 10 because the framerate isn't as high, but they more than make up for it with the beauty and detail. Thankfully, the animation is top-notch when it counts, and incorporates some amazing Sakuga scenes. Also the OP dancing animation is godlike, even if you don't watch the donghua I highly recommend watching the OP and ED. (fun fact: the entire OP was animated by a single person, taking 3 months)

Sound: 10
The OP is a bop(and it's in english) and the ED is FIRE, and the insert songs for each arc are quite nice as well. The BGM does a great job of setting the atmosphere of a scene and bringing out the emotion during important moments. From triumphant to tense to poignant, It was actually composed by Tenmon, who also worked on 5 Centimeters per Second as well as some of Makoto Shinkai's early works. The overall styles consist of lo-fi/chill, shounen hype, pop ballads, orchestral, and my favorite, sad piano.
The voice-acting is done very well, and you can hear a characters personality and emotion through their tones. However, the dubbing is chinese, which could be jarring to some, but I feel that it more accurately portrays the character's emotion as it's not overdramatized.

Characters: 9
The 2 main characters are very intriguing so far, and their backstories are revealed little by little throughout the series. Shi Guang Dai Li Ren or Link Click is a popular Chinese cartoon about time travel. This has earned it many comparisons to Stein’s Gate, but beyond the time travel element this isn’t really a good comparison. Speaking of time travel, the plot of Link Click was problematic within China since the CCP banned all time travel fiction in 2011. It was considered “disrespectful to history and unhealthy wish fulfilment”. So instead of time machines, Link Clink had to create a truly bizarre and unique magic system to get the series approved by Communist Party censors.

The series is about these 2 best friends named Xiaoshi and Guang who run an unusual business together. If they look at a photo, Guang has the power to know everything the person in the photo did or thought within a 12-hour period. Xiaoshi has the power to project his consciousness back into the past and take over the body of the person in the photo for 12 hours. Guang can then use his natural telepathy to tell Xiaoshi what to do in the past. Yes, Guang’s telepathy is so strong he can communicate with people in the past, even if they’re located vast distances away. They don’t actually try to change the past, but rather use their power to learn information. In the first episode, an unknown client gives the duo the task to recover the financial data of a powerful CEO who is suspected of fraud and embezzlement. Xiaoshi goes back in time 48 hours to take control of the CEO’s female secretary and has to find a way to read the data off the guy’s laptop. That’s a typical episode of Link Click until they meet their main antagonist later in the series.

So how does Link Click compare to Stein’s Gate? Stein’s Gate is about a goofball mad scientist who accidentally creates a time machine by putting bananas in an old microwave oven. This immediately gets him targeted for assassination by the real-life research agency CERN, who in the world of Stein’s Gate is just HYDRA from Marvel Comics. They’re an evil organization of ex-Nazis who want to take over the world using super tech and succeed in 99.999 percent of all timelines. The plot of Stein’s Gate is fucking stupid. The series works because of its over the top characters and how they interact with each other. Stein’s Gate combines goofy humor with serious drama in a very VN sort of way. It is 100% a visual novel adaptation and it succeeds by embracing that.

Link Click is kind of the polar opposite. It’s a series that lives and dies by its plot and high concept. The series succeeds because the plot is cool and the way they do time travel is unlike anything we’ve ever seen before. It’s not particularly funny. It’s not there to generate dozens of memes and reaction gifs. The characters aren’t as fleshed out or memorable as the ones in Stein’s Gate. Xiaoshi is the funny man and Guang is the straight man. Xaioshi has a heart of gold and Guang is kind of a cold bastard who is all about profit with little concern for the people in the photographs. By the end of the first episode, you basically know who these guys are. Link Click is less like a visual novel and more like a Hollywood blockbuster. I would say it’s a lot closer to Inception than it is to Stein’s Gate.

One thing that actually impressed me about Link Click was its willingness to address China’s massive wealth gap and rural poverty that still persists to this day. This isn’t something that China typically advertises, so I assume this show was mostly made for a Chinese domestic audience. This may seem like a minor thing, but it shows up in Link Click repeatedly and actually makes the story more powerful while also making it feel more real.

Link Click was made entirely in Shanghai by a studio called BiliBili. Apparently, it was made using Adobe Flash, so that’s actually really impressive. They made a God Tier rated anime in 2021 using the same software that brought us immortal classics like “Kerrigan & the Frog” 20 years ago.

Is Link Click worth watching? Absolutely! However, if you don’t have Funimation, you might have a rough time. On most of the streaming sites as of this review, some of the episodes use the official subs and some use the fan subs. The fan subs are bad. REALLY bad! I’m getting flashbacks to bootleg Naruto DVDs where they translated “dattebayo” as “Fuck!”. I think most MAL users that watched Link Click just watched it in the original Mandarin, but if you’re a gweilo and don’t have a Funimation subscription, be prepared for a rough ride.

Overall (9/10)

From the first episode of Shiguang Daili Ren (LINK CICK), there has been something special about it. It balances its gorgeous art, voice acting, soundtrack, and interesting premise with well-built characters and engaging storytelling. Shiguang Daili Ren stands out from the crowd and moves the Chinese donghua market forward. First we got Mo Dao Zu Shi (The Grandmaster of Demonic Cultivation), then we got Tian Guan Ci Fu (Heavent's Official Blessing), and now, it seem like it's Shiguang Daili Ren's time to shine.

Story (9/10)
The premise is quite straightforward. Three young adults run a photography shop, and sometimes they get special clients who want to alter something in their past in one way or another. Lu Guang has the power to help Cheng Xiaoshi enter these photos and helps him achieve the client's goals while not altering the present timeline. That is, until the present will inevitably be changed for the sake of plot. Though simple, it's captivating. Each individual story involving separate clients also has their specialties, with emotional impact involved in every episode. You really get to connect with these characters that you'll likely only see in two episodes at most. So far, many of these stories have centered around the everyday lives of those with humble beginnings in the countryside. In any case, by the 5th episode, the main plot has begun to move forward, involving the main characters in the present timeline more. Every episodes brings about a sense of nostalgia and empathy for the stories and characters; it never gets boring.

Art (10/10)
With a light, watercolor-esque feeling, the art is truly exceptional. It uses realistic coloring and rather gritty-yet-clean line art, almost like fine chalk. The animation is also exceptional. In any case, it's absolutely beautiful.

Sound (8/10)
The OP and ED go hard, a mix of Chinese and English rap/pop. The OST on the other hand is pretty standard, but it works perfectly well.

Character (9/10)
Each character has their own past, though the three main characters' pasts and backstories are yet to be discovered. Cheng Xiaoshi and Lu Guang, through the small snippets we have been shown, seem to have intriguing pasts and personalities that aren't based on any clichés, making them very natural and relatable. Thanks to their realistic personalities and a between their partner dynamic, it gives for a very enjoyable watch. As of episode 5 actually, surprisingly, the supporting cast may be the star of the show! Every client is a unique and charming character in their own right.

Enjoyment (10/10)
My eyes are glued to the screen when watching Shiguang Daili Ren. Of course, there are imperfections to this donghua for sure, but there is never a dull moment in this show despite the "normalcy" in its characters and visuals in comparison to the many low-budget, low-quality animes we've been seeing as the anime market becomes oversaturated. It's a breath of fresh air, and I am excited and proud to see more Chinese donghua make their way into the mainstream!
