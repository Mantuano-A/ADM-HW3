The Promised Neverland 2nd Season is a brilliant achievement of Japanese Television. It is a series that has no reason to exist other than to profit off of unsuspecting fans of the first season. This is the spiritual successor to Tokyo Ghoul: Root A—another anime original sequel that attempted to inflict as much severe pain and suffering on the manga fans as possible. Except, at least that blatant marketing scheme had semi-decent audiovisuals.

This sequel follows Female Kirito, previously known as “Emma”. Similar to Kirito, Emma is also the most powerful player in the video game. Rather than using a sword, she has the power of plot armor to protect her from any danger! Getting chased by a demon? She can outrun it. Armed soldiers can’t kill demons with guns? She’ll kill it with a bow & arrow. Instead of getting followed around by a harem, Female Kirito has a bunch of children that go along with anything she says. They have names but don’t worry—you won’t remember them anyway. Previously a character known as “Ray” challenged Emma’s overly idealistic goals. This season he has been renamed to “Hay” because has no weight on the plot and allows Emma to do anything—no matter how stupid she acts. A long-lost friend (who was lost for less than 6 episodes) returns, but he’s actually a bitter Bad Guy now! Shocking! But why is he bad? He explains everything to us because we are incapable of comprehending storytelling—we must be fucking idiots!

Just sit back and get spoonfed by one of Season Two’s many iconic exposition scenes: Emma and her harem gather in a non-descript room, and one character stands-up and summarizes 100 chapters of the manga in a five to ten-minute long speech. It’s like karaoke night, but instead of singing, they monotonously read SparkNotes summaries of the manga with no animation whatsoever. Unless you consider a PowerPoint presentation to be animation. The Promised Neverland 2nd Season does not tell a story, nor does it do much of anything. It uses a bunch of unrelated plot points throughout dozens of chapters. Years of time skips pass by in minutes. Then it stitches these barely connected pieces together like a hideous quilt.

The entire show rides on how Emma’s feeling today. In one episode she’ll let the kids eat potentially poisonous fish, in the other she’ll happily help a demon with its groceries despite knowing they like to eat children for supper. No matter anything she does, she’ll have either a smile on her face or a slightly concerned expression. I don’t like comparing the adaptation to the manga, but in this case, it is impossible to not notice the differences in art detail and writing quality. All of the passion is drained, turning climactic moments into laughably bad dialogue. Villains double-cross each other in unison like a hive mind being controlled by an annoyed writer.

They should have explained the motivations behind everyone’s sudden changes in goals and morality, but that’d require hard work. And lazy writers don't like doing hard work. Why bother adapting three beloved story arcs from the manga—full of battles that would be VERY expensive to animate—when you can just summarize them with a PowerPoint presentation? That's easy money! Strip away the psychological horror, intelligence, atmosphere, and logic that made the original anime so special, and what do you have left? This piece of inane shit. Granted, I am not a huge fan of the original, but it was light-years better than this. The show is dependent upon the demon creature effects but, even in 2D, they are below average at best. It’s remarkable, really. CloverWorks has mastered the art of being lazy, and they should be applauded for that.

My instinct is to blame the writers at Cloverworks, but it’s clear from disappearing names in the credits that they did not want to be associated with this colossal dumpster fire. According to interviews, the mangaka co-wrote the anime original story, but that has never stopped a film from being bad. George Lucas co-wrote The Rise of the Skywalker, and George R.R. Martin worked on the clusterfuck that was Game of Thrones S8. Just like those adaptations, The Promised Neverland will go down as one of the greatest declines in writing quality in all of anime history. The last time I saw an anime crash and burn so hard in the final act was Darling in the FranXX.

That’s all there is to The Promised Neverland 2nd season. Finishing as quickly as possible to cash a paycheck. Every aspect of it is rushed: The stiff voice performances, the recycled soundtrack is poorly mixed. They reuse the same facial expressions, character animations, background art, and CGI monsters. Even to the untrained eye, it will be obvious the production has cut corners in literally every aspect. If you don’t care about quality and want your intelligence to be insulted, watch this remarkable landmark in anime history. Come along, join these personified potatoes on a journey to rebel against Weekly Shounen Jump by becoming the most profitable adaptation while putting in the least amount of talent & budget possible. A journey which they have failed, apparently, according to the abysmal sales.

A butchered adaptation, neglecting to include fan favorite characters from the manga and removing entire arcs, complete and utter disregard of the great tone and atmosphere that was so carefully crafted in the first season, and rushing a once engaging story to high hell to make it a sloppy mess.

This is horror. This is The Promised Neverland Season Two. And it's frickin brilliant.

Now you may be sitting in your comfy chair, mouth agape in shock, and asking yourself "Is this guy quite possibly insane?" Probably. But I also had a revelation to something that you don't know...yet. But that's ok. I'm here to enlighten you dear reader. I came to realize that this season's apparent monumental failure was in fact done on PURPOSE. Yeah, I know it sounds unbelievable, but let me lift the veil from your eyes so you too can begin to appreciate this anime's efforts.

Let me ask you something. What is the primary genre of Promised Neverland? The one that enticed viewers to watch and kept them craving more? The one that propelled this series to the height of popularity? It's horror. THAT is the driving appeal of this series. The first season did an excellent job with this. I'm sure you don't need me to reiterate the solid pacing, timing, character growth, conflict, and more. But most importantly, it was a faithful adaptation to the source material.

Now imaging destroying all of that.
The once well defined and centralized plot has become convoluted, characters have become one dimensional, and the solid build up has just completely evaporated among other things. But also...taking an anime original approach!? Gasp! Now this is what I call true horror!

There's only so far you can go when trying to instill fear into viewers through animation. Studio Cloverworks clearly knew that they had to take things to the next level, so instead of providing some exterior unnerving content like they did with the first season, they strike directly at the heart of fans by making them feel the utter despair of watching something they love turn into...this. Wow. Totally soul crushing. And absolutely genius!

And that's why this season is clearly a subversive masterpiece. Shows of this genre aim to break the viewer through deep and dark content. Yet Cloverworks found a way to effectively do this through an entirely different approach, and it clearly shows. Seriously, the overwhelming animosity towards this season by fans is almost as scary as this show's complete and utter implosion. I applaud them for their enlightened and forward thinking methods! But here's a warning. This only applies if you read and were a fan of the manga. Otherwise, it's just a incredibly lackluster sequel to a really good first season. Through watching, fans get to experience the purest, most rage inducing kind of horror there is.

The horror of a butchered adaptation.

I debated whether it was even worth writing a review for the sham that is Yakuneba season 2 for about an hour or so before deciding that after all the time, energy and think pieces I’ve given this series, I should write one.

Let me be clear, season 2 of Yakuneba, or TPN, whatever you prefer, is the biggest disappointment I’ve ever seen in all my years of watching anime. Those familiar with the manga will know that the content after season 2 has an astronomical drop in quality, but season 2’s material was the best in the series. Yet, for some unknown reason, Cloverworks and the author decided to rewrite the story and condense the entire second half of the manga into 11 episodes and remove one of the best characters in the series. Why? There’s been rumours that the author didn’t like the pacing of Goldy Pond(the arc that should have been adapted this season) and decided to rework things. Out of all the things that went wrong in the second half of the manga, Goldy Pond was not one of them.

Now that I’ve got my manga reader tirade out of the way, I’ll explain just how atrocious this season was. “Well, I didn’t read the manga, so I might like it still, right?” No. Even if you were fortunate enough to not read the final half of Yakuneba’s manga, you will still find plenty of faults with this season. For one, the pacing is comically bad. Conflicts are set up and resolved in the span of a few minutes, often with huge leaps in logic. For two, the kids are often put into situations for which they have no training or previous experience and yet able to magically work their way out of with no casualties or so much as a scratch. For three, none of the mystery and tension that the first season or first half of the manga had are present. There’s never a need to fear for anything because you know Emma will talk no jutsu her way out of things.

This season is just bad, not just because it butchered a fan favourite arc or cut out a fan favourite character and moments. It’s bad because it’s poorly written, poorly directed and a shadow of the former glory of TPN. Honestly, I thought it would be hard to get worse than the manga, but Cloverworks succeeded here on that front. If you want a somewhat mediocre conclusion to the story, check that out, but even then don’t expect much. Yakuneba S2 gets 2 dumpster fires out of 10.

***THIS REVIEW CONTAINS SPOILERS***

Whenever a second season of an established franchise makes its return, I’m sure viewers will have a checklist in their heads. I’m talking about the type of checklist about content we want to see and those we won’t see. Because let’s face it, there’s no such thing as a perfect adaptation. Every anime has flaws and The Promised Neverland (TPN) season 2 is no exception. But when we talk about flaws, let’s just say that season 2 bit off a bite more than it can chew.

With no recapping of events, we are right where we started from the conclusion of the first season. That’s right, Emma and the kids are free at last from the terror and scheming Isabella. The one they knew as “Mama” had a dark secret and now that she knows what the kids know. Thus, the cat-and-mouse game begins in the TPN world. Freedom tastes very refreshing from Grace Field House. For the first time in their lives, Emma, Norman, Ray, and the children are able to take charge of their own lives. But being free also comes at risks. Let’s not forget that TPN’s outside world has demons, the primary antagonists of the franchise with a desire to consume humans. Will the kids survive in the outside world or will they become flesh and food?

Watching this second season feels exactly what it wants you to think: a cat-and-mouse game where the kids are the mice and the demons are the cats. The kids not only have to evade demons but also must learn to survive in the outside world. Because let’s face some ugly facts: they’re not adults. Sure, there are characters who think like adults such as Norman but none of them truly have the experience of a survivor. The outside environment is a dangerous hazard of its own. Every day, these kids must find food, shelter, and means to keep each other sane. Despite being free, they are far from being safe as everyday tells a new chapter in their lives. Anything could go wrong whether it’s being caught by the demons, facing the threat of food scarcity, or even being betrayed by one of their own. It’s one of the more engaging storytelling part of the show as every episode brings a new development. The first few episodes shows Emma and Ray being reunited along with other kids. It doesn’t take long for them to begin discovering some truth about the past of the TPN world. That is thanks to two demons they encounter, Mujika and Sonju. You may think all demons are evil but in their own words, they take a different approach at surviving and refraining from consuming humans. Regardless, it’s important for the kids to learn as much as about the world as possible.

One of the primary objectives of this season is for the kids to find a mysterious character named William Minerva. It is theorized that Minerva may contain valuable information about the outside world and perhaps even the key to their future. As each episode progresses forward, we learn more about the demons thanks to Norman’s return and his own discoveries. But at some point in this season, the kids run into internal problems of their own. It seems Norman himself has a plan to take down the demons but Emma has other thoughts. In her words and ideology, not all demons are evil or need to be eradicated. More importantly, Emma realizes that Norman is carrying too much burden on his own. In one of the climatic episodes, we see how Norman struggles to carry his plan and the violence that occurs in a town with demons. It highlights the personal struggle of Norman and what he perceives as the ‘right’ or ‘wrong’ thing to do. TPN Season 2 takes on this approach of characterizing its established cast. It takes a deeper dive into their personalities and shows how actions have consequences. Unfortunately, I can’t say this sort of characterization is well done. In general, Norman’s actions speaks for itself and from the start, he knew what would happen if his plans were carried out. Emma’s ideal clash shows the opposite spectrum of the two sides. Regardless who you agree with, both characters should know that every action has a cause and effect. In exploring these character emotions, it doesn’t really develop either character on a mature level although it does an adequate job at showing their personalities. And it’s sad for me to say, but if you’re in looking for character depth with other cast members, this isn’t the season for you. Outside of Emma, Norman, and Ray, most of the kids are treated more like fodder.

What season 2 does promises is the way it sells its survival thriller story. Let me just say that while the characters aren’t the best actors of this show, the story itself is engaging with mystery, psychological, and thriller content. The Outside World built by the creator is fascinating that feels alive with the dangers of the environment and of course, the demons. Returning to this season are those monstrous creatures hunting for their food, or in this case, the escaped children. It’s more than just a cat-and-mouse game. It’s about survival. It’s about facing the future. And it’s about how kids can take back what they’ve once lost: their freedom and identity.

Now, I am an anime only when it comes to the Promised Neverland, however the choice to not include fan favorite arcs from the manga (i.e. Goldie Pond) and characters such as Hugo in this season just seems like a slap in the face to the fans of this show. However, even being an anime only viewer its hard not to sympathize with the manga readers as the season rushes plot points, uses an embarrassing amount of plot armor, and can't even invoke a single emotion out of the viewer besides "meh". Finally, the entire use of psychological suspense and horror that made the first season so lovable is just gone.

Art: 2
Let me state something before I get into this section, I'm a fan of Cloverworks as they have animated some personal favorites of mine, but WOW did they drop the ball with this project. From the use of abysmal CGI for some of the wild demons in this show to the use of stills during action scenes, its almost comically bad. Compared to the first season, you can see the lack of time that Cloverworks had to work with the second season, as the characters look dull, the demons almost look lifeless and not in a good way, and lastly the overall dark color pallet that falls onto the show just makes the setting and characters look bored. Even the wildlife in the show which are supposed to make the world more vast are animated in the same bland fashion as everything else in this show.

Sound: 7
The sound of the show is one of the few saving graces for the second season. I find the opening and outro to be really well done and sort of play in the childlike dreams of the escaped children, especially the opening which as an almost uplifting feel to it. Also, I found the OST and a lot of the background noises for the environment to be realistic and immersive enough to draw you away from the horrid art. The noises some of the wild demons make, though cheesy, are pretty well done and definitely remind the viewer of the beasts that these children are facing in the wild. Also, the sounds show the savagery of the wild demons which shows a solid contrast between the wild demons and the civilized or in the context of the show, non-degenerated demons.

Character: 5
The show starts with some very solid character development that peaks the viewer's interest, specifically in Emma after she kills an animal after hunting, but unfortunately, we have yet to see this matured Emma throughout the duration of the season. Also, I'm not a huge fan of Ray's arc as I never really saw him as a yes-man to Emma, but I guess it does stay consistent to the ending of the first season once he decides to go with Emma's plan. One development I really liked was Norman's, but that to was shutdown very fast in favor of moving the plot along, as his character comes full circle in the span of two episodes. Seeing Norman's determination and will to make a better world for his family no matter the cost was refreshing to the viewer, but once again that simply just did not fit the story of the show and was literally just forgotten and ignored after Norman enacts his plan. Isabella also has a stupidly written arc, but I guess it does make sense in terms of the foreshadowing from not only this season, but the first. I think a lot of the character arcs in the second season vary from ok to really good, however almost all of them fall into the ok tier because of the rushed pacing of the second season.

Enjoyment: 4
The second season of Promised Neverland has a lot of mountains and valleys in terms of enjoyment for me. The first 3-4 episodes were all very immersive and kept that feeling of the first season, however after that when the story began to stray off into plot armor and rushed pacing, is when the show gets terribly boring. Most of the episodes following those first couple of episodes are bland, unimportant, and most specifically just stupid. The reintroduction of Norman and "death" of Norman are basically 7 episodes apart from one another, which just shows how rushed this second season is. However, episode 8 was very good in my opinion, and it was honestly a new feeling and vibe that the series has yet to give off, but this was very short-lived in favor of the boring story which has unfolded in front of us. Its a below average enjoyment for the viewer, with moments of phenomenal writing and scenes, but the mid to borderline terrible episodes outweigh these great moments and episodes for me.

Overall: 3
It sucks to say that the Promised Neverland has went the Tokyo Ghoul route, yet here we are. This season honestly feels like a cash-grab; an attempt to profit off and milk the fanbase of the show that was cultivated off the first season and manga. The decision to cut some of the best parts of the manga was a bonehead decision, but ignoring that fact is the glaring truth that the show is just bad. There's no stakes because of the plot armor, all the characters seem to fall in line with Emma's plan, the art makes a lot of the "cool" scenes fall flat, and much more. The first season of the Promised Neverland was something truly special, a new-age show that had a brand new plot and lovable characters that seemed to prey for the writers, as the first episode had a death. Unfortunately, the second season just can't hold a candle to the manga or the first season, as it struggles to build off the lighting caught in the bottle by the previous season and manga chapters.
