There is nothing quite like the raw feeling of enjoyment that a viewer can savour throughout their first sitting of a work that manages to mesmerize them.

Nonetheless, when approaching the charms and disillusionments of any show, our vision is rarely able to cope with the bigger picture, and to prove a point we must, more often than not, focus our attention in the specifics. In those details that for some might be of scarce relevance, but that in great measure have the potential to make a difference, both in a good and a bad direction.

There is really no other way to start referring to Gosenzo-sama Banbanzai! than by mentioning its unique conception of staging. In this matter, the setting is eminently static and simple, most of the times simulating an actual theatrical mise-en-scène. This becomes particularly noticeable thanks to the certain use of props, the blatant repetition of trademarks, the measured use of lightning and noted visual gimmicks such as superposition. Admittedly the elements are not many, yet they are consistently deliberate.

The role of the characters fits consequently, when even the setting gets a thematical importance, their existence consists of several layers. Are they actors? Are they puppets instead? Or are they simply the people the story is telling us they are?
As it turns out, all the previous options are correct, giving them room to question what is happening from various perspectives. Their actions and expressions are clearly dramatized, with abundance of gesticulation; and, narratively speaking, the use of soliloquies to directly refer to the audience. Their appearance, closer to that of puppets, is more of a themed importance and reflects in the same roles they're playing.

Put together in the late 80s by Studio Pierrot, this production is noteworthy for being the result of a collaboration from many interesting names from the industry. Heading them all is a pre-Ghost in the Shell Mamoru Oshii, directly afterwards his role with the 1st Mobile Police Patlabor OVA alongside with the rest of the Headgear group. This series presents us with his more comedic manner of scripting, which might be not the most popular of his facets, but certainly one worth of recognition. The musical scope is composed by Kenji Kawai.

When it comes to old school OVAs, odds are you're not precisely going to be delighted with what you see. They won't ever be the paragon of originality and they frequently got discontinued midway. Gosenzo-sama is a refreshing proof that this was not always the case, and will remain as an entertaining option for those who don't mind to dig a little bit deeper.

Thanks for reading.

Gosenzo-sama Banbanzai is an anime I would not recommend to just anyone. It's an obscurity that won't be easy to appreciate. From its directing, it is similar to what Kunihiko Ikuhara first introduced in Revolutionary Girl Utena. From its execution, it reminds FLCL, but also goes into such levels of abstractity, it comes close to animated shorts such as 'Nekojiru Soup' and 'Inaka Isha.' I'd only ever recommend it to experienced viewers and old schoolers.


The anime starts off by a doorbell ringing. Followed by a 10 minutes long debate over who should open the door. When it's finally opened, a person claiming to be a timetraveler is standing behind the door and declares she is the future granddaughter of the house owner's son. This is accepted by the father, and by the son (upon prove), but not by the mother. This breaks the family apart, and starts off a story so tragicomedic it could as well be originating from ancient Greek.


An aspect similar seen in titles such as Zaregoto, Yojouhan Shinwa Taikei and Jinrui -where the main characters are nameless personas such as I, Watashi and Boku- is introduced. What Babanzai does is on whole other dimension, however. It creates a character called "Audience", which is -you guessed it- the viewer itself. With this, the series starts addressing all sort of issues related to family, life, society, future, past, and even to issues such as how helpless it is for the characters to take controll of their own lives when they come to acknowledge that they are indeed puppets controlled by the creator of this anime. Is it okay just to give up? Maybe the viewer can offer some help?


In case this review got your interested, a word of warning. This series took me off guard so hard I had to watch it twice just to make sure I didn't miss anything. In case you plan on trying it, make sure to come with an open mind and mindset ready for a different type of adventure.

Idiosyncratic and unfairly forgotten, a six-part OVA "Gosenzosama Banbanzai!" (literally, "Long Live the Ancestors!") belongs to the most experimental period in Oshii’s career. Told in the form of a loony play, this farcical tragicomedy revolves around the Yomota family: a teenage boy, Inumaru, his father Kinekuni and his mother Tamiko.

Their normal life starts to collapse after the arrival of a mysterious girl, Maroko, who claims to be Inumaru’s granddaughter from the future. Even though she has a hereditary star-shaped birthmark on her buttock, Tamiko refuses to acknowledge her as Yomota. So, she leaves her husband and son, and hires a private detective, Bannai Tatara, to reveal the true identity of the uninvited guest.

Driven by self-irony and absurd, slapstick humor, a twisted, paradoxical story is directed in the vein of the French New Wave masters, with a short metaphorical mockumentary in which different kinds of birds and humans are compared, at the beginning of each episode.

An otherwise linear narrative rooted in its own logic is deconstructed, as Oshii plays with both the characters’ and the audience’s expectations. However, his anarchic shenanigans are not meaningless and they do follow some rules – no matter how strange it may sound.

Utterly aware of the fact they are nothing but marionettes controlled by a merciless puppeteer, the protagonists still try to manipulate the events in order to turn the situation to their own advantage. Occasionally, they burst into “karaoke soliloquies” under the spotlight – and not only figuratively speaking. With the most essential props and the minimalist backdrops on one hand and medium-related "deviations" on the other, "Gosenzosama Banbanzai!" is simultaneously stagey and filmic.

After all this spectacle you are left wandering through this labyrinth of ideas, hoping to find the answer to a question asked since the beggining of human life: "Who are you, only a puppet?"

Gosenzo-sama Banbanzai!

This was a very interesting watch. I'd heard people saying it's a "taxing watch" or not suitable for casual viewing but i believe that it's perfect for both casual and critical viewing and that's exactly what was on Mamoru Oshii's mind when he decided on the screenplay. What I mean by it is the fact that the show is presented as a stage drama. Much like Sheakspeare's plays, this show is an artistic commentary on a subject while also being a compelling and enjoyable narrative.

It’s a commentary on the typical japanese family with poor family bonding conveyed through the three characters designed as marionettes fitting a certain archetype.
Almost two third of the first episode is spent showing the seething discontent the three of them have. The entrance of Maroko was nothing more than a catalyst. Her actions had nothing to do with the family breaking apart, she just gave everyone an excuse to let out all their feelings.This is where the aforementioned theatrical screenplay becomes a plot point. It isn’t just a setting, the audience and the author are in a sense, characters in the show and all the other characters are aware that they are merely part of a drama where they are to fulfill their roles. This can be interpreted once again as a commentary on people sticking to traditions and following them due to which everyone lives a harmonious life. But they aren't satisfied with this and therefore break the harmony or better, in Inumaro's words, "seek out their own story". This view is further strengthened by the mini documentaries at the start of each episode. They show that as long as one goes with their role, everything works out fine.

The show also discusses biased gender roles and deeply ingrained traditions in general. Inumaru doesn't let Maroko work even though he can't provide for them alone. Tamiko comes back to her family after all the discontent because it's in their culture for the wife to be with her family and try to make things work.
There is also mockery of the ancestor worship culture in Japan where ancestors are highly regarded and worshipped when they were just ordinary or even bad people.

All in all, the takeaway from the show is that sticking to traditions isn’t the best thing but straying too far from your roots can have negative implications. This message is conveyed with elegance through the long soliloquies and memorable musical performances.

Before starting with the anime review , there is something which I want to talk about. This might sound pretentious but it will give my best impression about how I feel about this anime. You can skip the first two paragraphs.

There is no doubt that anime is a media for storytelling (yes some anime can be solely about animation with no story to tell). The same is for live action movies and shows. Both media have their advantages and disadvantages. For example , Anime as animation can show probably anything as long as it can be drawn unlike live action which is limited. One the other hand , live action has the advantage of dynamic camera movement which is hard and costly to replicate in animation ignoring the initial cost of the physical camera and setup. Some shots are still hard to do in a live action setting . It is often in anime where characters in the background aren't drawn impressively with details unlike in live action where you just need to hire actors for the backgrounds. It is safer to animate dangerous scenes unlike using stuntmen in live action. Anime and live action can be impressive by excelling at what they are better at doing. There is often a criticism of some anime saying that this anime can be better if it was live action but sometimes anime and live action movies can be more impressive by imitating other media at what is harder for them even at a cost. With the advance of CGI , motion capturing and etc , live action can do pretty impressive things that would only look great on animation in the past. Lord of the Rings is impressive from a technical point of view. It is probably more impressive than animation at what it did. It was also one of the first movies to use AI for CGI characters for those impressive war scenes. There are a lot of impressive anime from a technical point of view such as Steamboy with its dynamic camera movement in some shots.

If you survived the previous paragraph , take this one XD . you can see that I didn’t mention elements which affect visual storytelling because both can have the same underground work and techniques suitable for both. Cinematography is transferable to both media. Live action uses real cameras while anime uses a virtual one. Yes, their limitations dictated what they can do. I talked about anime and live action but what about live performances such as Plays. Ok this is an interesting one for me. Plays in theatre have the biggest limitations and differences in comparison with Anime and live action. It is constrained by physical settings , so one can’t just use multiple cuts because changing the setting requires effort and time which can’t be afforded. There is no camera to move or to focus on a character , so they have to use the spotlight on the actor and sometimes the actor is facing the audience to show facial expression. I believe that the limitations of the plays as a media gave it a new different experience than anime and live action. For example , the tower in Rapunzel can be easily drawn and Live action movies can use green screens but for a play , they can’t use a real tower, so a toy model is used. This gives a surreal feeling to the scenes. It is common in plays to display information by info dumping if it can’t be shown. For example , messenger is a common character trope used to provide information. Awkward time skips are also used because it might be impossible to display those information except through exposition. Characters' monologues can feel like talking to the audience, breaking the 4th wall,and etc. Plays have their own appeal because of such limitations. Because of such appeal and its significance in the history of storytelling , it is often a source of inspiration for a lot of directors and authors including the anime industry. Gosenzo-sama Banbanzai! is an experimental anime from 1989 which mixed both anime and theatrical plays to create a strange experience.


Gosenzo-sama Banbanzai! is a mix between both anime and theatrical plays. What do I mean ? Gosenzo-sama Banbanzai! is divided into several acts with a transition between them . Every act is focused in a single stage as if it is an actual play. A lot of non-diegetic sounds are used as if there is an audience like cheering and even Sieg Heil. The characters/actors are facing the camera a lot aka the audience instead of other characters. There is a spotlight used in some scenes on the actors. Some characters introduce themselves while facing the audience. The setting in the fifth episode got morphed as if it is a stage. There is no doubt that the anime tries to mimic theatrical plays. There is still a virtual camera to direct the audience focus and some scenes which can’t be in a play.

What is Gosenzo-sama Banbanzai! about ? It is an absurd comedy family drama in which its absurdity is used to give a meta commentary about storytelling. So it is a story about stories as well, including commentary about the difference between life and common story structure, the search for believability in stories, philosophical expositions while making fun of it ,and etc. The anime used coca cola product placement as a foreshadowing tool. One way to create suspension of disbelief within the audience is to remove the masquerade about what they are watching. It is a show which makes fun of its premise and has fun with it while telling a story about the meaning of family and its place in society. The basic premise without spoiler is Maroko Yomota, granddaughter of Inumaru (young son of the Yomota family) traveled back in time to visit her ancestors but the family began to collapse due to her introduction in the story. Btw she proved that she is a part of the family by showing a JoJo Star birthmark on the ass. There is nothing graphical in the scene.


Gosenzo-sama Banbanzai! commentary on stories doesn’t stop at the structure but extends to the actors. The characters are fleshed in this way. Inumaru Yomota (the son) wants to take control over his story in his desired direction. Kinekuni Yomota (the father) is bored from his life and stressed from the debts to the degree of accepting Maroko’s story. Tamiko Yomota (the mother) is the pragmatic character who wouldn’t believe the over the top story of Maroko. Tamiko Yomota and Kinekuni Yomota are the polar opposite complementing the story. They have a dimension to their character related to how we accept/perceive stories as well as characters in the play.

While the story holds elements related to time travel, and it would create plot holes in any normal story but in the case of Gosenzo-sama Banbanzai! , plot holes are not a concern because it isn’t the focus. The story for sure knows that it is unbelievable and it used it for its themes. A smart thing the anime did was to increase the scope of the events by having Maroko’s action and time travel being a futuristic social phenomenon, Thus the story is now about society as well and making Maroko a more believable character in this absurd story while being the least developed one in the family. She is a one-note character to the degree of being funny in a silly way.

To be honest , not everyone goes and watches theatrical plays because of its unique appeal but because of how great the actors are in performing their role. Gosenzo-sama Banbanzai! voice actors did a great job at using their voices to create comical situations. The sudden change in the tone and volume of the voice and over the top acting is a common and still effective way to deliver comedy. The anime also creates comedy by subverting the expectations in some scenes. It is funny how Inumaru Yomota is persisting to ask for money even in situations which don't really fit in the first episode. My favorite section in the anime is the intro to each episode. It is a documentary style part about birds in wildlife with information which fits the theme of the story. They also use human anime characters as actors to represent the birds. It is the inverse of Anthropomorphism and it looks funny. I also got to know a weird fact about ostrich. I will not talk about the rest to prevent spoilers.

The anime style is inspired from theatrical plays which gives it an appeal but on the other hand it means that it inherited some of the theatrical plays limitations and imposes those limitations on it. This anime has parts of its story told through expositions and info dumping and constrained in a stage for a long duration within the episode. This can be boring to some viewers including me because I prefer the “Show, don't tell” method for storytelling which can’t be done in theatrical plays for some events. The story climax has been shown in episode 5 but the conclusion is far less satisfying in episode 6. This can be attributed to the huge time skip with a lot of the events being just told to the viewer in the end. The frustrating part is that the anime doesn’t fully commit to the idea of mimicking theatrical plays, so the ending might be the better time to do so.


The first two paragraphs are long and might not be necessary but those are ideas in my mind which I wanted to write about. They also give out my reasons for why I would recommend this anime to anyone who just wants to watch something different after experiencing typical anime for a long time. The score in this review doesn’t reflect on my opinion about how great the show is or how much I enjoyed it but how much I recommend it. This anime can be your favorite anime if you like its style and themes.

I hope everyone is doing great or having a good time.

In Gosenzo-sama Banbanzai, Mamoru Oshii is intent on informing his viewers that the nature of family relations, taboos, laws, the value of money, the value of products, and most things really, are all unverifiable fictions that we collectively agree to invest in for the sake of maintaining a functional civilization. To the extent that we can individually choose to believe whatever we want, to interpret things how we may, to imagine our own stories for ourselves, we have absolute freedom. However, insofar as we're beholden to the scrutiny of others and to the tangible consequences of our actions, civilization's collective beliefs bear on us as individuals. Some of these collective beliefs are useful, and some, like the value of commodities and money, ideas of happiness, honor and freedom that are always dangled in front of us and never truly attainable, only serve to turn us against each other, even against ourselves, and lead us to squander relationships we should cherish dearly.

Inomaru is wrapped up in fantasy, and paralyzed by a fear of reality falling short and hurting him, so he sits back and observes, and daydreams, and waits for something to come to him and change things. Kinekuni (the father) paid for a home for his family thinking it would make them happy, but it costs so much that he doesn't get to do anything but work. He's always too busy, or too tired to enjoy his time with the family he bought the house for. Tamiko (the mother) is trapped in a "supporting role," and seems to feel anxiety, not over the work of being supportive, but over being looked at as a person in a "supporting role," or in other words, not the main character. Additionally, it isn't clear how receptive Inomaru and Kinekuni are toward her efforts, even though she is their mother and wife, respectively, simply because she lacks the superficial charms of a girl like Maroko (the granddaughter). Fantasies of happiness, ideals of beauty, of what a woman should look like, what a family should be like, what a happy family's house is like, etc. and the endless pursuit of these culturally produced fantasies and ideals, are precisely what prevent the Yomota family from functioning as a family and fulfilling each other.

Since we're on the subject of fantasy and narrative, Oshii takes the opportunity to reflect on our relationship with Fiction with a capital F, as in the stories in novels, films, even OVAs perhaps... What is a good story? Why do stories appeal to us? I honestly have little idea what Oshii thinks on this matter despite him speaking through the characters about it at length, except that we as subjects are attracted to the opportunity to witness a beginning and an ending to something, or someone, since we are unable to experience our own beginning and ending.

I'm actually quite interested in the topics that are under discussion in this series, but I don't know that this is the method by which I would like to have been imparted Mamoru Oshii's ideas about them. It's a little dorky, and a little condescending to subject viewers to the types of monologues that the characters spew in this anime. More than half of the time, the story is not of remote concern. The story is merely an excuse to talk, and an example of a story for Oshii to remark on through the characters. It's not a totally uninteresting story, but it's not given interesting treatment.

I'm never deceived into thinking I'm watching a story unfolding between and around people. It's like I'm watching a D.A.R.E. skit, where I know the actors just put a little scenario together to illustrate their point, as a supplement, with the intention of frequently breaking, speaking directly to the audience and explaining what's happening; to make sure I really understand how bad it is to do drugs even one time, no matter if a cool friend or stranger tries to convince me otherwise. The interactions and dialogue are highly stylized (If that word is even appropriate here. What I mean is that no one talks the way the characters do, which is basically true of all dialogue in fiction, but in this case, the dialogue doesn't even have much to do with who the character is. They're all just speaking directly for Oshii in a way that's vaguely centered on their assumed concerns as individuals, but could just as easily have been said by any other character in the show with slight modifications) and unnatural. There's no illusion. I know exactly what's happening, and what Oshii is trying to get me to understand is just the content of the words being spoken at me.

I feel like this anime is an actual example of what Monogatari fans often mistakenly purport Monogatari to be like: A bunch of absurd dialogue and action that challenges taboos and is filled with thoughtful, deconstructive metanarrative commentary. The relationships and character interactions are actually more believable and coherent in Gosenzo-sama than what I've seen of Monogatari, somehow, and there's no senseless, transparent pandering (how stupid it would be to dump a bunch of pointless ecchi into an anime that highlights problematic cultural illusions like Gosenzo-sama). The problem is, in practice, this extreme focus on commentary and deconstruction gets in its own way by detracting immensely from the show's formal efficacy, aesthetics, and the overall... enjoyability of the experience of watching an anime. Just make your point by letting characters that feel somewhat like real people talk to each other, come into conflict with each other, and so on. Don't give me the play by play. I would like to be able to recommend this to all those enlightened "but it's fine because I can tell the difference between fiction and reality" types on the anime discussion boards, but there's not much chance they'll actually enjoy it considering how self-important and preachy it is, much less understand what's being expressly stated about the narratives we create about ourselves, and the fictions we chase and adhere to like our lives depended on them.

The best part of each episode is the bird biology lesson intro, except 6, which unfortunately doesn't have one. At least those parts are actually funny (and yes, they're obviously allegorical for what is to follow in the episode).

Some other things to look out for:

Yellow/Blue stuff, characters/objects suddenly drenched in yellow/blue light. Yellow is clearly, repeatedly associated with Maroko, and you might say that when characters turn yellow, or objects that are yellow (the getaway car) show up, it's indicative of their being held in Maroko's sphere of influence. They've submitted themselves to a version of the narrative wherein Maroko is the main character.

Cameras & Camera Brands. Tatara is not merely a detective. He's the man behind the camera, like Oshii himself, and he speaks in part from that perspective. He frames the images people see, manipulates the story.
