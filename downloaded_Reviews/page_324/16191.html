As of the first half of what I presume to be the first season, Painting The Community (Jianghu is a metaphorical concept typically used to describe the community of martial artists, although literally, it means rivers and lakes) At Night is hardly centered around racing. Instead, it is quite a ridiculous story about various gangs and secretive corporations fighting over control of a commercial district among other things, and one of their ways to settle disputes it through racing. I assume, based on the plot development, the main protagonist would eventually go into racing, which he aspires to. But that's not what this season would be about. A more accurate picture might be if cut scenes of games like Need For Speed or Grand Theft Auto were extended into an anime, replete with comedic local mob bosses in a lawless town. Another way to put it is a Durarara!! that's less focused on the supernatural and high school, and more focused on the crime. The protagonist, after all, runs a business to steal cars at the start of the show.

This isn't Initial D nor Capeta, nor is it pretending to be.

To be sure, the graphics do look like upscaled PS2, or perhaps first generation PS3 ports, so if that bothers you, there's no need to continue. However, I can offer a reason to watch it. While the plot is quite cheesy at first and, to be honest, remains so, the characters gradually develop, baring their insecurities and motivations, into a likeable cast. The comedy is typical for a Chinese show, but well timed. Unlike the tiresome manzai duo comedy of Japanese anime where a funny-man does something stupid, and a straight-man points out the obvious in a funny way, Chinese comedy is more slapstick, based around silly puns and misunderstandings, or making fun of everyday situations. I much prefer this style, and although Painting The Community At Night is never laugh-out-loud funny, it can be pretty amusing.

Even though I say the plot is ridiculous, honestly, it's never as bad as fan favorites like The Great Pretender or Hinamatsuri. In fact, I dare say the plot holes are quite typical, such as plot armor when flurries of bullets miss or the protagonists inexplicably escaping from entire enemy gangs. If you're okay with all the inconsistencies in Tokyo Revengers, then you'd be okay with this. The arcs feature the protagonists pulling off disguises and thwarting illegal business meetings and such, all of which is very unrealistic, but not unlike numerous Japanese shounen. There's even a throwback to that first race in Initial D where the protagonists overtake the other team in the final hairpin curves down a mountain road. The explanations here aren't as technical for sure. It's not for car lovers. But the physics are somewhat reasonable. The training is somewhat believable. There's no magic like Air Gear or Skate Infinity.

The background polygon count isn't great, but the texture mapping isn't completely lazy. The character movements are somewhat stiff, but the choreography doesn't suck. At least the fights have punches and kicks that are parried and blocked in a reasonable way, despite the Hollywood neck snapping. But it doesn't utilize utterly lazy punch against punch, or kick against kick non-choreography of the likes of DBZ.

I'm not saying that it's great. It's not a paragon of donghua. It's not a must watch. However, it does enough right that it's decently enjoyable, if you could get past the 3D CGI.
